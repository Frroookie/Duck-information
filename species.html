Common duck species

A number of duck species that I commonly see here at the Philippines and ducks that I see in the internet.

Mallard

In the Philippines, ducks are often seen in wetlands, rice fields, ponds, and coastal marshes. Many local duck species feed on grains, aquatic plants, insects, and small invertebrates. They are especially common in the early morning and late afternoon, where they gather in peaceful groups near water.

Wood Duck

One of the most colorful ducks in North America. Males have an iridescent green and purple head with white stripes, red eyes, and a multicolored bill. Unlike most ducks, they nest in tree cavities near water rather than on the ground.

Mandarin Duck

Native to East Asia and often considered one of the most ornate ducks in the world. Males have an orange "mane," sail-like orange feathers on their sides, and a bold patterned face. They're a symbol of fidelity in parts of East Asian culture.

Northern Shoveler

Named for its oversized, spoon-shaped bill, used to filter tiny food particles out of shallow water by swishing side to side. Males have a green head and rusty-orange sides — a bit like a mallard that visited a costume shop.

Bufflehead

One of the smallest diving ducks in North America. Males appear to have a giant puffy white patch wrapping around the back of an otherwise dark, iridescent head. They nest in old woodpecker holes.

Callduck

A small domestic duck breed known for its rounded body, friendly nature, and bright, often colorful plumage. They are commonly kept as ornamental birds and are easy to recognize because of their compact shape and lively, curious behavior.

Harlequin Duck

A striking sea duck found along cold northern coasts, famous for its bold black, white, and chestnut pattern. Males are especially bright and dramatic, while females are more muted but still distinctive on rocky shorelines and fast-moving streams.

Muscovy Duck

A large, mostly black duck with patches of white and distinctive red, warty skin around the face (caruncles). Originally from Central/South America, now also common as a domesticated farmyard duck worldwide.

Spotting tip: males ("drakes") are usually the colorful ones — females tend toward duller brown patterns for camouflage while nesting, so don't expect both sexes of a species to look alike.
